## A Thirteen-Year Employee and the Challenge of Sustained Performance

Aleksandr Akulov joined Bloomberg LP on June 11, 2012. Over the course of his tenure, he built meaningful client relationships in the ETS-Listed AMER South group, where he covered institutional clients, managed a portfolio of accounts, and was measured -- like every member of his team -- on revenue generation, client activity, strategic product adoptions, pipeline development, and campaign engagement. For many years, he performed well. His manager acknowledged as much in the 2025 year-end evaluation when he wrote: "Alex historically has proven that he has the skill set to be successful in the group." That sentence was not a concession. It was the foundation of the evaluation's message: the company believed in Aleksandr, believed he could do this job, and wanted him to get back to the standard he had previously demonstrated.

Bloomberg LP is a company that invests in its employees. It paid for Aleksandr's MBA/JD degree. It provides one of the most generous parental leave policies in the financial services industry: six months of fully paid leave for a primary caregiver, available equally to fathers and mothers regardless of gender. Bloomberg provides manager training on leave administration, and the industry standard for handling coverage during leave is temporary reassignment with full restoration upon return. When Aleksandr took his first parental leave from approximately November 2023 to May 2024, and his second from July 12, 2025 to December 28, 2025, Bloomberg honored that policy completely. He received his full salary for both leave periods. His position was held for him. He returned each time to his role, his title, and his team.

This case is not about parental leave, and it is not about national origin. Aleksandr took leave. Bloomberg paid him. He came back. What happened between those events was a performance conversation -- the kind that happens to employees every year across every team in the company. The plaintiff asks you to believe that an honest performance assessment is retaliation, and that informal workplace greetings constitute ethnic discrimination. The evidence shows otherwise on both counts.

## The Performance Concerns Were Real and Pre-Dated the Second Leave

Before Aleksandr left for his second parental leave on July 12, 2025, his manager conducted a standard mid-year evaluation covering January through July of that year. That evaluation identified specific, measurable shortfalls that had nothing to do with parental leave and everything to do with how Aleksandr was working when he was on the job.

The mid-year evaluation found that Aleksandr had booked only one GDSF forecast during the first half of 2025 -- a single deal at Leverage Shares totaling $26,300. By the midpoint of the year, that was his entire bookable revenue production. He had three credited strategic campaign adoptions, but those three users had each tried the relevant workflows exactly once and had not actually incorporated them into their processes. His manager rated these adoptions as "generous" credits because the behavior they were intended to drive never materialized.

Most significantly, the manager identified a pattern of insufficient client engagement that had been flagged in prior evaluation periods. Aleksandr's primary coverage was in Connecticut, one of the densest financial services corridors in the country. Yet in the first half of 2025, he made only 11 client-visit days in Connecticut, conducting 45 in-person meetings across just 18 accounts. For an outside sales role where coverage breadth and face-time frequency are core competencies, these numbers reflected inadequate effort. His days on the road typically began around 10:00 AM and ended before the market close, cutting short the window for client engagement. The manager had raised this exact concern -- the need for more complete days and a broader range of client visits -- in the prior year's evaluation. Aleksandr had not made the changes.

These are not manufactured criticisms assembled after the fact. They are documented, specific, measurable performance observations recorded in Bloomberg's evaluation system before the second leave began. The mid-year evaluation was written before Aleksandr went on leave. It assessed performance during a period when he was working full-time, compared against peers who were also working full-time over the same January-to-July window. The leave did not cause these shortfalls. The shortfalls existed while Aleksandr was present, active, and working every day.

The mid-year review timing presented a practical challenge. The employee was on parental leave, and Bloomberg's standard practice is to conduct performance reviews as substantive in-person conversations, not perfunctory phone calls. Rather than intrude on the employee's leave with a remote performance discussion -- which could itself be characterized as interfering with protected leave -- the manager made the decision to post the administrative mid-year notation and defer the substantive performance conversation to the employee's return. The year-end evaluation, delivered in person with both the manager and his manager present, served as the comprehensive feedback session that covered both mid-year and year-end performance. The employee received detailed, specific feedback on every dimension of his performance, with concrete examples and clear expectations going forward.

The plaintiff will point to Bloomberg's internal performance evaluation page, which states that employees on extended leave require "individual assessment" rather than peer comparison, and that interim review conversations are "required." The plaintiff has a screenshot of this policy. The defense must address this directly. Bloomberg's written policy provides guidelines for handling evaluations during extended leave, and those guidelines were substantially followed even if the specific procedural steps were not executed in the precise sequence contemplated. The manager did conduct an individual assessment of the employee's performance during the period he was working. The year-end evaluation reviewed the employee's own metrics, his own activity, and his own output during the first half of the year. The evaluation's references to peer performance were contextual, providing the benchmarks that any sales performance assessment requires. As for the "required" interim review conversation: the manager prepared and posted the mid-year evaluation in the system. The substantive conversation was deferred to the employee's return, at which point the year-end evaluation served as the comprehensive review session. Whether a deferral satisfies the "required" interim conversation guideline is a question the defense must be prepared to address with specifics about how other managers handled extended-leave evaluations across Bloomberg. If the policy was routinely interpreted to allow deferral until return, the manager's approach was consistent with institutional practice. If other managers conducted interim conversations during leave, the manager's failure to do so is a procedural gap that the plaintiff's attorney will exploit. This is a known vulnerability. The defense cannot claim "we followed standard evaluation procedures" without accounting for these two specific policy provisions.

## The Year-End Evaluation Was the Only Honest Option

When it came time to write the 2025 year-end evaluation, the manager faced a straightforward practical reality: the only period during which Aleksandr worked and could be evaluated against his peers was the first half of the year. He was on approved parental leave for the entire second half. The alternative to evaluating his first-half performance was to not evaluate him at all -- to simply skip the assessment, give him a pass, and pretend the year had not happened. That would have been a disservice to Aleksandr, to his peers, and to the integrity of the evaluation process.

The year-end evaluation stated plainly what had happened: "Alex spent the majority of the second half of 2025 on paternity leave (7/11/25-12/28/25), as a result we were only able to evaluate his performance relative to his peers at the midyear." This was context, not causation. The manager was explaining why the evaluation was based on the data it was based on. He was not penalizing Aleksandr for being on leave. He was explaining that the available performance data came from the period when Aleksandr was working -- the same period covered in the mid-year evaluation, the same period when every member of the team was present and could be compared fairly.

The plaintiff's legal team argues that the year-end evaluation compared Aleksandr's half-year output against peers who had a full year. That mischaracterizes what the evaluation says. The evaluation explicitly states it assessed performance "at the midyear" -- meaning the comparison was against peers' midyear performance, not their full-year totals. Every team member was evaluated at mid-year. Aleksandr's midyear numbers lagged his peers' midyear numbers. The fact that those peers went on to produce additional results in the second half does not retroactively improve Aleksandr's first-half performance.

The plaintiff will argue that Bloomberg's own evaluation policy requires "individual assessment" for employees on extended leave rather than peer comparison, and that the year-end evaluation violated this policy by benchmarking the plaintiff's output against teammates. The defense must acknowledge that peer comparison language appears in the evaluation, but the comparison was limited to the midyear period when all team members were equally present. Assessing whether an employee's output meets role expectations inherently requires some reference point. The evaluation used peer midyear performance as that reference point, which is a reasonable methodology even under an "individual assessment" framework. The distinction between "we compared him to peers" and "we assessed his individual output against role expectations, which are informed by team benchmarks" is the line the defense must hold. Whether Bloomberg's policy language supports that interpretation is a question the defense should resolve by reviewing how other extended-leave evaluations were handled across the organization.

The plaintiff also takes issue with the evaluation mentioning parental leave at all. But not mentioning it would have been stranger. The employee was absent for six months. A year-end evaluation that assessed the full year without noting why the employee was present for only half of it would have been incomplete and potentially confusing to anyone reviewing the record. The manager included the context because it was relevant context -- not because it was the reason for the assessment.

The plaintiff's attorney has reportedly criticized the manager for building a "data-based case" rather than relying on subjective assessments, arguing that the use of verifiable metrics creates an opportunity to challenge specific numbers. This criticism is revealing: it acknowledges that the evaluation was grounded in measurable performance data, not subjective bias. The manager cited revenue figures, client visit counts, adoption metrics, and pipeline data because those are the legitimate performance criteria for the role. An evaluation built on data rather than opinion is exactly what responsible performance management looks like. If any individual data point was imprecise -- a forecast count that omitted a stage, a visit count that used a different methodology -- those are the kinds of marginal discrepancies that occur in any multi-product, multi-metric evaluation system. They reflect the complexity of the data, not manipulation of it. The plaintiff's attorney's preferred alternative -- that the manager should have used only subjective, unfalsifiable criticisms -- is an implicit concession that data-grounded evaluations are harder to characterize as pretextual.

## Bloomberg's Compensation Process Is Rigorous, Not Rigged

The plaintiff claims that Bloomberg's compensation process works in reverse: pay is set first, then evaluations are written to justify the predetermined outcome. This mischaracterizes a multi-stage process that involves independent inputs, institutional checks, and systemic constraints on individual manager discretion.

Bloomberg's annual compensation cycle is not a single manager typing a number into a spreadsheet. It involves manager evaluations, peer calibration sessions, and algorithmic inputs. Calibration sessions are standard corporate practice across the financial services industry, designed to ensure consistency across teams. In these sessions, managers present their assessments of direct reports and those assessments are tested against peers' performance within and across groups. This is quality control. It exists because companies that allow managers to evaluate in isolation produce inconsistent, biased outcomes. Bloomberg's process is designed to prevent exactly the kind of unchecked managerial discretion the plaintiff elsewhere alleges.

The plaintiff's characterization of the manager "voting against" employees during calibration is a distortion of what calibration sessions do. Managers advocate for their team members where performance warrants it and identify where team members need improvement where it does not. Honest assessment is the purpose of calibration. A manager who identifies an underperforming team member during calibration is fulfilling the function the process was designed for, not weaponizing it. The plaintiff's framing treats the normal functioning of a quality-control mechanism as evidence of bias -- which would mean the process can only work correctly when it produces outcomes the plaintiff approves.

The algorithmic component of Bloomberg's compensation system further constrains manager discretion. Compensation is not set by a manager's whim; it is bounded by data-driven inputs that ensure outcomes are proportional to performance across the organization. This is precisely the kind of institutional safeguard that prevents the arbitrary punishment the plaintiff alleges. A manager who wanted to target a specific employee's compensation would need to overcome systemic constraints that apply to every compensation decision across the company.

The skip-level manager's role in reviewing outcomes is an additional check, not a second liability point. Rich Santore reviewed both the evaluation and the compensation outcome and found both appropriate. Santore was present at the February 17 evaluation meeting. He had independent access to the performance data. His concurrence represents a second institutional actor validating the assessment on its merits. The plaintiff's suggestion that Santore's involvement compounds the problem rather than constraining it would require evidence that Santore participated in an effort to target the plaintiff -- evidence the plaintiff does not have.

The evaluation was written to document the performance assessment, not to justify a predetermined conclusion. If the evaluation's data points were selectively chosen to make the employee appear worse than he actually performed, the CRM records, the activity reports, and the revenue systems will demonstrate that. The defense must be prepared to show the evaluation's methodology: which metrics were cited, how they were sourced, and whether the same methodology was applied to every team member's evaluation. If the methodology was consistent, the "reverse-engineering" claim fails. If it was not, the defense must explain why.

## The Counter-Metrics Tell an Incomplete Story

The plaintiff will present aggregate activity numbers showing that Aleksandr had 648 client interactions (described as the highest on the team), 178 visits (described as the highest), and 177 unique firms engaged (described as the highest). These numbers, sourced from Bloomberg's standard team reporting tools, measure volume. They do not measure quality, strategic value, or outcomes.

Bloomberg does not evaluate its sales professionals solely on how many phone calls they make or how many firms they contact. It evaluates them on whether those interactions produce results: revenue bookings, sustained product adoptions, pipeline that converts to bookable forecasts, and strategic client development. By those measures -- the measures that actually drive the business -- Aleksandr's first half of 2025 was not consistent with the expectations of the role.

Consider the specifics. His 648 interactions and 177 unique firms generated limited bookable revenue at the mid-year mark. His three strategic adoptions resulted in single-use trials that were never repeated. His Connecticut coverage -- the primary territory assigned to him -- showed insufficient breadth given the density of opportunity in that corridor. High activity with low conversion is not high performance. It is an indication that something in the approach is not working, which is exactly what the evaluation identified.

The plaintiff will raise the number of GDSF forecasts in the CRM system, claiming the evaluation undercounted. Revenue forecasting in Bloomberg's system involves multiple stages, products, and qualification criteria. The evaluation's characterization of bookable forecasts reflected the methodology used consistently across the team -- distinguishing between qualified, bookable pipeline and earlier-stage entries. If any forecast was miscounted, it was an oversight in a multi-product, multi-stage pipeline, not a deliberate suppression of the plaintiff's numbers. The defense must verify the exact CRM records and be prepared to explain the counting methodology that was applied to all team members equally.

The plaintiff's prorated revenue analysis -- claiming approximately $80,100 in METC revenue plus $39,511 in RFQe revenue for a total of $119,611 against a full-year target of $115,000, or 104% of target achieved in approximately six months of active work -- requires additional context. The METC total includes $53,800 in Two Seas revenue that was booked while Aleksandr was on leave, from a deal that his pre-leave pipeline work contributed to. The company has always acknowledged that revenue, and it was credited to him in the evaluation. But at the actual mid-year mark, when Aleksandr was present and being compared against peers who were also present, his bookable revenue consisted of one deal for $26,300. It does not change what the midyear snapshot showed about Aleksandr's performance relative to his peers at that point in time.

The defense must acknowledge the total revenue figure honestly. If METC and RFQe revenue are combined, the plaintiff exceeded his full-year target while working approximately half the year. The defense's position is that the evaluation assessed midyear performance, not full-year totals, and that revenue recognition timing (particularly the RFQe booking in January 2026) followed standard product-line procedures. But the narrative that an employee who produced 104% of his annual target in six months was an underperformer is extremely difficult to sell to a jury. The defense must focus on the metrics that genuinely showed shortfalls -- adoption quality, pipeline breadth, day structure -- rather than anchoring the underperformance characterization to revenue, where the numbers now favor the plaintiff.

The plaintiff will also allege that $39,511 in RFQe revenue he generated in 2025 was booked in January 2026 instead, and that this booking decision was made by the manager to suppress his numbers during the evaluation period. The plaintiff claims that if this RFQe revenue is added to his $80,100 in METC revenue, his real 2025 output was $119,611 against the $115,000 target -- 104% attainment, exceeding his full-year target while working approximately half the year.

Revenue recognition timing in electronic trading sales follows product-specific booking rules, not individual manager discretion. RFQe revenue, like other product-line revenue, is booked according to Bloomberg's standard revenue recognition policies, which govern when a transaction qualifies for credit. Revenue earned late in a calendar year routinely books in January of the following year due to processing cycles, reconciliation periods, and fiscal cutoffs. This is a mechanical function of Bloomberg's finance systems, not a managerial decision made at the team level. If the approximately $30,000 in RFQe revenue booked on or around January 4, 2026, followed Bloomberg's standard recognition timeline for that product, the timing was procedural, not punitive.

The defense must verify this immediately. Bloomberg's finance and revenue operations teams maintain booking records that document when revenue was recognized, under what product code, and according to what recognition schedule. If RFQe revenue generated in Q4 2025 routinely books in January for all team members across the organization, the plaintiff's allegation collapses. If the booking of this specific revenue was an exception to the standard pattern, the defense must identify the legitimate business reason and document it.

The activity metrics and the prorated revenue calculation are exactly the kind of selective framing that obscures rather than illuminates. An employee can lead the team in call volume while lagging the team in every metric that the business uses to evaluate success. The evaluation was based on the full range of performance criteria, not a subset chosen to favor a particular narrative.

The defense must be careful here. The timing is extremely damaging: the rebuttal was delivered Friday March 13, and the counting change occurred approximately four days later. If the manager cannot demonstrate that the methodology clarification was planned before the rebuttal arrived, the temporal proximity speaks for itself.

The defense's position is that adoption metrics can be counted at different levels of granularity -- initial signup versus sustained usage, single-trial versus recurring engagement -- and that the manager clarified the counting methodology to better reflect the business objective of sustained product adoption rather than one-time signups. If only the plaintiff's numbers were materially affected, this is very difficult to defend.

## Coverage Decisions Were Business-Driven, Not Retaliatory

When an employee takes a six-month leave of absence -- for any reason -- client relationships cannot be put on hold. Clients need coverage. Markets do not pause. The team must continue to function. During Aleksandr's first leave (approximately November 2023 to May 2024), certain Connecticut accounts were reassigned to James Johnston to ensure continuity of client service. Those accounts developed working relationships with Johnston during the six-month absence. When Aleksandr returned, a business judgment was made about how to reintegrate his coverage. Some quality CT clients that had been serviced by Johnston during the absence remained with Johnston, reflecting the working relationships that had developed. No outright territory was stripped after the first leave; the adjustments were account-level, not geographic.

The more significant coverage changes occurred after Aleksandr's second leave return in late December 2025. Houston, Albany, and Tennessee territories were reassigned to Patrick Hiller while Aleksandr was present and working. These were not coverage continuity decisions made during Aleksandr's absence -- they were affirmative reassignments made after his return. The defense must be candid about this distinction. The "operational coverage during absence" rationale that applies to first-leave account shifts does not apply here because Aleksandr was on the job when these territories were taken. The defense's position is that these reassignments reflected a broader team reorganization driven by evolving team capacity, geographic alignment, Patrick's addition to the team, and business priorities. Coverage rebalancing is a routine management function that occurs regularly, not only when an employee returns from leave. But the defense must produce specific business justifications for each territory's reassignment -- why Houston went to Patrick, why Tennessee went to Patrick, why Albany went to Patrick -- and those justifications must be documented, not retroactively constructed. The plaintiff will emphasize that all three went to the same person (Patrick Hiller, who has 2x Aleksandr's coverage plus Boston), and that no territory was reassigned in the other direction.

This same principle applied to the redistribution of Bryan Baptist's accounts when Baptist left the company during Aleksandr's second leave. The team was managing active client relationships under reduced headcount. Coverage from Baptist's departure was distributed based on each remaining team member's existing geographic footprint, current capacity, active deal flow, and client relationships. Aleksandr was on leave and had no active client relationships during the distribution period. His manager was working with the team members who were present and actively covering clients. This was a practical staffing decision made under real-time operational pressure, not a deliberate exclusion.

The plaintiff will argue that the Baptist redistribution also included inherited forecasts and active pipeline, meaning recipients closed deals they did not originate and those inherited revenues inflated their subsequent performance numbers. This is how sales teams function: when a team member departs, their active deals must be assigned to someone who can service the client and close the business. The revenue from those inherited deals is still Bloomberg revenue. It must be credited to someone. The team members who received those accounts also assumed the work of servicing them -- the deals did not close themselves. Aleksandr was not available to service accounts or close deals during this period because he was on leave. The redistribution went to team members who were present and able to do the work.

Regarding the Connecticut lead that was assigned to another team member: leads are routed based on existing client relationships, deal context, and who is best positioned to convert the opportunity. A single lead assignment is a routine management decision. It does not constitute evidence of a conspiracy.

The February 24, 2026 exchange about the Clear Street account illustrates normal management oversight, not retaliation. Clear Street was a new account that Aleksandr proactively developed -- he identified the client, secured a demo, and brought it to his manager's attention. The manager assigned it to him during their one-on-one meeting. When the manager later noticed the assignment in the CRM and did not recall the conversation, he asked a routine clarifying question. Upon confirmation, the manager confirmed his support for the coverage ("Happy for you to cover them... which is great since I think they trade a lot of ETFs"). His suggestion about the "switch" product was a coaching observation -- helping the employee identify a cross-selling opportunity with a high-volume client. This is precisely the kind of proactive management guidance the evaluation encouraged. The plaintiff characterizes the manager's momentary confusion about the assignment as an attempted strip, but the exchange ended with the manager affirming the coverage -- which is the opposite of retaliation.

The plaintiff emphasizes the difference in coverage universe size between Aleksandr (1,138 firms) and other team members, particularly James Johnston (6,099 firms). This comparison, while numerically accurate, strips away every relevant variable other than raw count. Coverage universes are not fungible. The firms assigned to each team member differ in size, product usage, revenue potential, geographic density, and relationship maturity. Aleksandr's Connecticut territory encompasses a concentrated financial services corridor where a single account can generate significant revenue. A team member covering a geographically dispersed territory with thousands of smaller firms has a larger count but not necessarily greater revenue opportunity per assigned firm. Coverage is assigned based on product specialization, geographic alignment, client tier, historical relationship ownership, and demonstrated capacity to service accounts -- not leave duration.

The 5.4x ratio between Johnston's firm count and Aleksandr's does not account for any of these variables. It is designed to produce outrage, not understanding.

## The March 2026 Account Assignments Were Legitimate Business Actions

In early March 2026, the manager made several coverage assignments to the plaintiff, including ETF-oriented accounts, a broker-dealer client previously worked by a junior team member, and FNY, a firm seeking to set up a meeting. The plaintiff characterizes these as "sham coverage restoration" -- junk accounts assigned to pad his portfolio on paper while the real account (AB) was stripped. This framing is wrong on every count.

Start with what actually happened. The AB account was reassigned to another team member on January 21, 2026, approximately three weeks after the plaintiff's return from leave. Account reassignments during extended absences are routine -- as discussed above, clients who have been serviced by another representative for six months develop working relationships that cannot simply be reversed. The reassignment of AB to Pat reflected the practical reality that Pat had been managing the relationship during the plaintiff's absence. This was a client-continuity decision, not a punitive one.

The March assignments, by contrast, represent the manager's ongoing effort to rebuild the plaintiff's coverage. ETF-focused accounts were assigned because electronic trading is a core product line for the team and the plaintiff's stated area of focus. The manager specifically noted that these accounts "trade a lot of ETFs" -- directly relevant to the plaintiff's coverage responsibilities and revenue targets. When a broker-dealer client needed a new coverage representative, the manager assigned it to an experienced team member (the plaintiff, with nearly fourteen years of tenure) rather than leaving it with a junior employee (Tommy) who was still getting established. This is the opposite of dumping problem accounts -- it is a recognition that a veteran salesperson is better equipped to handle a challenging client relationship. FNY requested a meeting, and the manager routed the opportunity to the plaintiff with the suggestion that the firm's pods "trade a lot of ETFs, worth investigating." A manager directing an inbound client inquiry to a team member is standard account assignment, not evidence of retaliation.

The plaintiff's complaint amounts to this: when accounts are removed, it is retaliation; when accounts are added, it is sham restoration. There is no management action that the plaintiff would characterize as legitimate. The removal of AB was a client-continuity decision made during a coverage transition. The addition of ETF accounts, the broker-dealer, and FNY were business-appropriate assignments that expanded the plaintiff's coverage universe and revenue opportunity. A manager who removes coverage is accused of stripping; a manager who adds coverage is accused of padding. This framework leaves no room for any management decision to be interpreted in good faith.

The plaintiff will cite a former colleague's characterization that the manager gives employees "shitty accounts." That is one person's subjective opinion about the quality of coverage assignments, expressed in an informal text conversation during her own acrimonious departure. Whether an account is valuable depends on how it is worked, what products the client needs, and what revenue can be developed. Sales professionals routinely convert overlooked or challenging accounts into meaningful revenue. A veteran employee with the plaintiff's tenure and claimed skill set should be equipped to develop any account assignment -- and his own counter-metrics claim he leads the team in client activity. An employee who leads his team in client interactions but dismisses new assignments as "junk" is selectively presenting his own engagement.

The defense should document the revenue potential and trading activity of each assigned account. If the ETF accounts do in fact trade ETFs, if FNY's pods have product needs that Bloomberg can serve, and if the broker-dealer generates commissionable activity, the plaintiff's "sham" characterization fails on the facts. The CRM data will show what these accounts actually produce.

## James Johnston Is Not a Comparable Employee

The plaintiff frames James Johnston as a controlled comparison: same team, same manager, same role, similar tenure, but Johnston took one month of leave while Aleksandr took six months, and Johnston has 5.4x the coverage. The implication is that the only variable explaining the coverage gap is leave duration.

This comparison omits essential context. Johnston's coverage universe reflects years of client development, territory growth, and demonstrated capacity to manage a large book. If Johnston's coverage exceeded Aleksandr's before either employee took any parental leave -- and Bloomberg's historical records will show whether this is the case -- the disparity has nothing to do with leave and everything to do with the different trajectories of two careers that happen to share a team.

Additionally, Johnston's one-month leaves created minimal operational disruption. A four-week absence can be managed with temporary coverage arrangements that do not require full account reassignment. A six-month absence requires substantive redistribution. The business impact of a one-month absence and a six-month absence are genuinely, materially different, and managing those impacts differently is not evidence of discrimination -- it is evidence of responsive management. An employer is not required to pretend that one month and six months have the same operational consequences.

The plaintiff will argue that Johnston took short leave specifically because of fear of the manager's retaliation, not personal preference -- that Johnston observed what happened to the plaintiff after his six-month leave and chose to limit his own leave to avoid the same treatment. The plaintiff will further allege that colleagues have told the plaintiff they "feel bad" because they can see the retaliation occurring. If Johnston or other team members corroborate this -- that they chose shorter leave because they feared professional consequences -- it transforms the coverage disparity from a neutral business outcome into evidence of a chilling effect. The defense must be prepared for this framing. Bloomberg's leave policy provides six months of fully paid leave. If employees on the same team are self-limiting their leave usage because they fear managerial retaliation, that is itself evidence of a hostile environment toward leave-takers. The defense should not assume Johnston will corroborate the manager's position. If subpoenaed, Johnston's testimony about why he chose one month could go either direction.

## Revenue Targets Reflect Business Planning, Not Individual Targeting

Aleksandr's 2026 revenue target was set at $175,000, up from $115,000 in 2025 -- a 52% increase. The plaintiff frames this as a setup-to-fail: higher targets with fewer resources. The reality is that revenue targets are set through Bloomberg's standard business planning process, informed by market conditions, product line growth expectations, team capacity, and company-wide revenue goals. If other team members received comparable target increases for 2026 -- and the company's records will demonstrate whether they did -- the increase reflects organizational expectations, not individual persecution.

Revenue targets also reflect the employee's product portfolio, not just firm count. An employee with access to high-growth product lines or clients with expanding technology budgets may receive a higher target based on the revenue potential of their assigned accounts, regardless of how many firms are in the coverage universe. A single large client adopting a suite of Bloomberg products can generate more revenue than dozens of smaller accounts with limited product usage. The raw math of dividing a target by a firm count produces a misleading figure precisely because it treats all firms as equal, which they are not.

The plaintiff will raise a critical procedural point: the $175,000 target was never communicated to him. He discovered it by checking the METC screen himself. The manager was "very surprised" that the plaintiff noticed. This is significant because it undermines the defense's ability to frame the target as a jointly understood performance expectation. A target that was set silently, never communicated, and whose discovery surprised the manager looks like a metric designed to be failed rather than achieved. The defense must explain why the target was not communicated. If Bloomberg's standard practice is to communicate revenue targets to sales professionals at the start of the fiscal year (which is standard across financial services sales organizations), the failure to communicate the plaintiff's target is a procedural deviation that requires explanation. The defense should also address the comparator: Patrick Hiller has the same $175,000 target but with 2x the plaintiff's coverage universe plus Boston. If Patrick and the plaintiff share the same target despite Patrick having substantially more resources and territory, the plaintiff's argument that the target was set to be unachievable gains traction.

If the data shows that Aleksandr's target increase was disproportionate to his peers', that fact will need to be addressed. If it was consistent with team-wide or department-wide increases, the plaintiff's framing collapses.

## The Manager's Verbal Statements in Context

## Summary

This is a performance management case, not a retaliation case and not a discrimination case. An employee with a thirteen-year track record took extended parental leave, as was his right, and Bloomberg honored that right completely. When the employee returned, his performance during the available working period showed shortfalls in specific areas -- adoption quality, pipeline breadth, and day structure -- that the evaluation identified. The employee himself admitted to friends that he had deliberately reduced his effort. The evaluation reflected what the data showed and what the employee himself confirmed. The defense must acknowledge that the employee's revenue production ($119,611 total, 104% of his $115,000 annual target in approximately six months) undercuts the underperformance characterization on the revenue dimension. The case for underperformance rests on the qualitative metrics, not the revenue numbers.

The coverage decisions require honest differentiation. After the first leave, certain CT accounts were reassigned to Johnston during the absence and not fully restored -- a coverage continuity outcome, not a territory strip. After the second leave, three territories (Houston, Albany, Tennessee) were affirmatively reassigned to Patrick Hiller while the plaintiff was present and working. The defense must produce specific, documented business justifications for each second-leave territory reassignment because the "operational coverage during absence" rationale does not apply to changes made while the employee was on the job. The compensation decisions reflected the individual evaluation, but the defense must address the fact that the plaintiff was the sole team member with zero raise and zero bonus. Bloomberg's compensation process -- involving manager evaluations, peer calibration, algorithmic inputs, and skip-level review -- is designed to produce consistent, data-driven outcomes, not to enable individual targeting. The plaintiff's claim that the process works in reverse, with pay set first and evaluations written to match, must be addressed directly. If the defense can demonstrate the evaluation's drafting timeline and show that the same process governed every team member's outcome, this allegation fails. If it cannot, the allegation becomes a central vulnerability.

The statements attributed to the manager are the plaintiff's characterizations of conversations, filtered through an increasingly adversarial lens and organized by a support team that was building a lawsuit before the evaluation was even delivered. The "mostly cuz I took 6 months off" statement, if accurately reported, is the most damaging single attribution in the case file because it directly ties compensation to leave duration in the manager's own words. The defense must determine whether this statement can be denied or must be contextualized as a reference to reduced working-time output, and that determination depends on what the manager's own Bloomberg work phone records contain. Until those records are reviewed, this narrative cannot take a definitive position on what was said.

The national origin allegations -- "Comrade" and "Rahul" -- emerged for the first time on February 23, 2026, the same day the plaintiff was conducting intake calls with plaintiff's attorneys. These were informal workplace interactions that the plaintiff never objected to, never reported, and never mentioned to anyone during the year-plus they allegedly occurred. Their sudden recharacterization as discrimination coincides precisely with the plaintiff's litigation strategy, not with any change in the workplace environment. The manager used informal greetings common in high-pressure sales environments. If he adjusted his language over time, that reflects responsive management, not an admission of wrongdoing.

The plaintiff's supporting evidence has grown beyond what can be dismissed as a former employee's grievances and a friend group's manufactured narrative. The plaintiff now has: Bloomberg's own evaluation policy screenshot showing required interim conversations and individual assessment (both violated); an active team member (Emily Kruger) who heard the manager say the ranking was based on expecting the plaintiff to quit, who defended the plaintiff to the manager's face, and who independently challenged the IOI counting change; $119,611 in documented revenue (104% of annual target in six months); retroactive IOI methodology changes four days after the rebuttal; the manager framing base pay protection as a leave-tied favor; and the sole zero-comp outcome on the team. The CRM forecast closure and RFQe booking timing claims remain the plaintiff's account of system actions whose audit trails have not been independently reviewed; if those trails confirm standard procedures applied uniformly across the team, those specific allegations fail. But the structural evidence -- Bloomberg's own policies, the manager's own statements, an active employee's own real-time objections -- is substantially harder to dismiss. The chain of command theory ignores every formal complaint channel Bloomberg provides. The "Comrade" allegation was never raised through any channel until it was disclosed to a plaintiff's attorney.

The plaintiff has retained Phillips & Associates under a pre-litigation-only contingency arrangement, has delivered an attorney-reviewed rebuttal letter, and is expected to file an HR retaliation complaint before the March 30 follow-up meeting. The plaintiff has stated he wants to stay at Bloomberg. Bloomberg should take that at face value and respond accordingly: investigate any complaint thoroughly, address the rebuttal with documented facts, and continue managing the plaintiff's performance through the same process applied to every team member. The March 30 meeting will be audio-recorded by the plaintiff under New York's one-party consent law. Every statement the manager makes will become evidence. The defense must prepare the manager for this meeting as if preparing him for a deposition. The company's recent coverage assignments demonstrate ongoing investment in the plaintiff's portfolio and revenue opportunity. The rescheduled follow-up meeting demonstrates institutional willingness to engage, not obstruction.

Bloomberg values its employees. It values parental leave as a benefit and a right. It does not punish employees for using that benefit. It does not tolerate discrimination based on national origin or any other protected characteristic. What it does require -- of every employee, regardless of leave history or background -- is performance consistent with the expectations of the role. When performance falls short, Bloomberg provides feedback. That is what happened here. The plaintiff asks you to see retaliation and discrimination where there is accountability. The evidence does not support that conclusion.

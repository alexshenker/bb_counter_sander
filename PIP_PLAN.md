### 1.2 Timing: Distance From Leave Return

The employee returned from his second parental leave on December 28, 2025. His year-end evaluation was delivered on February 17, 2026. As of this writing (February 20, 2026), the employee has been back for approximately 8 weeks.

**Minimum safe timing for a PIP: No earlier than May 2026.** Rationale:

- Implementing a PIP within 90 days of return from protected leave (i.e., before late March) creates an extremely strong temporal proximity inference. Under NYCHRL's "motivating factor" standard, temporal proximity alone can support retaliation claims. A PIP in March would be roughly 60 days after return -- the timing equivalent of an admission.
- The year-end evaluation (Feb 17) serves as the documented performance feedback that precedes the PIP. The employee needs a documented opportunity to respond to that feedback and demonstrate improvement. A PIP implemented immediately after the evaluation denies that opportunity.
- The March follow-up meeting (approximately March 5-10) should be used to set specific interim expectations. If the employee fails to meet those interim expectations by late April or May, the PIP has a documented basis beyond the initial evaluation.

**Ideal timing: June-July 2026 at the earliest,** preceded by at least two documented checkpoint meetings (March and May) where specific expectations were communicated, support was offered, and performance against those expectations was assessed.

### 1.3 Documentation Baseline

Before implementing a PIP, the following documentation trail must exist:

1. **The February 2026 year-end evaluation** (exists). This provides the initial performance feedback.

2. **A written set of 2026 expectations** shared with the employee. These must be specific and measurable. The year-end evaluation's Looking Forward section is too vague ("focus on developing and closing new business opportunities across all product lines") to serve as a PIP predicate. The employee needs to have received a document that says "here is what you need to accomplish in Q1/Q2 2026, measured by these specific metrics." Sharing this with the employee during or after the March meeting creates the baseline.

3. **At least one documented interim checkpoint** (the March meeting) where the employee's progress against the expectations was reviewed, any shortfalls were identified specifically, and additional support was offered. This checkpoint must be documented in writing (an email summary from the manager or HR, not just a verbal conversation).

4. **A second documented checkpoint** (approximately May) showing continued shortfalls. This is the trigger point: the employee was given feedback (Feb), given specific expectations (March), given time and support, checked in on (May), and still fell short. The PIP follows from this documented sequence.

5. **Evidence that the same documentation cadence exists for other team members.** If the employee is the only person receiving documented checkpoint meetings and written expectations, the process itself is evidence of targeting. At minimum, the March and May checkpoint structure should be offered to or conducted with one or more peers.

### 1.4 Progressive Discipline: The PIP Cannot Be Step One

A defensible PIP is never the first step. The disciplinary progression must be:

1. **Feedback** (the evaluation -- completed Feb 17)
2. **Specific expectations** (to be completed at the March meeting)
3. **Support and coaching** (ongoing between March and the next checkpoint)
4. **Documented interim check-in** (approximately May)
5. **Warning that PIP is being considered** (may occur at the May checkpoint if warranted)
6. **PIP implementation** (June-July at earliest, if performance has not improved)

If the manager jumps from step 1 (evaluation) to step 6 (PIP), the employee's legal team will argue there was no genuine attempt to help the employee improve. The PIP was a predetermined conclusion, not a response to continued underperformance after receiving support.

### 1.5 HR Must Be Fully Engaged

HR must review and approve the PIP before delivery. HR involvement serves multiple purposes:

- It demonstrates the PIP was not a unilateral decision by a manager with alleged retaliatory animus
- It ensures the PIP meets Bloomberg's internal standards and legal requirements
- It creates a second institutional actor who reviewed the employee's performance and agreed a PIP was warranted
- It provides a witness to the process who is not the manager

HR should also review the coverage disparity data and the target. If HR signs off on a PIP with a $175,000 target and 1,138 firms while a peer has 6,099 firms and the same target, HR becomes complicit in the setup-to-fail narrative and the company loses the "one bad manager" defense.

**What this means for PIP design:**

1. **Any PIP revenue metric that depends on when revenue is "booked" is manipulable.** Platt controls the timing of RFQe bookings and CRM forecasts. If the PIP measures "revenue booked during the PIP period," Platt can delay bookings to push revenue past the PIP deadline, or accelerate bookings to front-load the period. 4. **The PIP needs independent revenue verification.** Before any revenue metric can be used, the company must establish a process where revenue attribution and booking timing are verified by someone other than the manager. If the manager is the sole person who determines when revenue is credited, the entire revenue component of the PIP is compromised.

5. **Activity-based metrics become more important than revenue metrics.** The RFQe manipulation allegation, combined with the CRM forecast tampering allegation, means that the two most direct revenue manipulation claims involve systems the manager can influence. PIP metrics should weight activity (client visits, onboards, unique firms engaged) more heavily than revenue. ## Section 2: The Model PIP

**This PIP should only be implemented after all prerequisites in Section 1 are met.** It is designed to be genuinely achievable for someone with 1,138 firms in the Connecticut corridor, with targets calibrated to the employee's actual resources, not the team average.

### 2.0 PIP Preamble

The PIP document itself should open with the following elements:

- **Statement of purpose:** "This Performance Improvement Plan is designed to provide [Employee] with specific goals, clear timelines, and dedicated support to address performance areas identified in the 2025 year-end evaluation and subsequent checkpoint meetings."
- **Acknowledgment of the employee's history:** "We recognize [Employee]'s 13+ years of service and historical contributions to the team." This is not a courtesy; it prevents the defense from later pretending the employee has always been a marginal performer.
- **Reference to the documented feedback trail:** Cite the evaluation date, the specific expectations shared at the March meeting, and the checkpoint meeting where continued shortfalls were identified.

### 2.1 Objective 1: Revenue Generation *(Updated March 2 -- tamper-proof metrics required)*

**Metric:** Total revenue attributed to the employee, verified by finance or sales operations (not solely by the manager). Revenue must be measured by trade execution or client payment confirmation, not by when a forecast is "booked" in GDSF or CRM.

**Target:** $45,000 in verified revenue over the 90-day PIP period.

**Justification:** The employee's 2026 annual target is $175,000 (a 52% increase over the confirmed 2025 target of $115,000 from Bloomberg's METC screen). This target has not been adjusted for coverage disparity, and the company should be prepared to justify whatever target is in effect at PIP implementation. A $45,000 quarterly target corresponds to an annualized pace of $180,000, which is in range of the $175,000 target. If the target has been adjusted downward per Section 1.1, the PIP quarterly target should be set at 25-30% of the adjusted annual target.

Critical note: If coverage has NOT been equalized by PIP implementation, the revenue target must be reduced proportionally. At 1,138 firms with a $175,000 annual target, a quarterly pace target above $25,000-$30,000 becomes increasingly difficult to defend as genuinely achievable relative to peers.

**How it's measured -- CRITICAL UPDATE (March 2):** Revenue cannot be measured solely through GDSF booking dates. The employee alleges two separate instances of the manager controlling revenue timing: (1) the CRM forecast tampering (~$53-58K Two Seas deal closed as "dupe" on September 5, 2025, and reopened), and (2) the RFQe revenue manipulation ($30K+ in 2025 RFQe revenue booked by Platt in January 2026 instead of 2025). The manager could delay bookings past the PIP deadline to engineer a failure.

**Revenue verification must therefore include:**

1. **Independent timestamp.** Revenue counted in the PIP should be measured by trade execution date or client payment date, not by the date a forecast is entered, closed, or booked in GDSF. 2. **Finance or operations confirmation.** A member of finance or sales operations (not the manager and not anyone reporting to the manager) should confirm the revenue attributable to the employee at each checkpoint. This removes the manager from the revenue verification chain.
3. **Real-time visibility for the employee.** The employee must have access to the same revenue data the evaluators are using. If the employee can see his own revenue in real time, any discrepancy between his view and the PIP evaluation is immediately visible and documentable.
4. **Inclusion of all revenue types.** Revenue from all product lines (EMSX, RBLD, RFQE, and any other sources) must be counted if the employee originated or serviced the relationship. The RFQe allegation specifically involves revenue Sasha generated that was booked after his evaluation period. If the PIP excludes certain revenue types or measures only a subset, the manager can steer high-value activity into uncounted categories.

**Peer benchmark:** The company must be prepared to produce the quarterly revenue pace for all team members during the same 90-day period, using the same verification methodology. If any peer with substantially more coverage fails to meet this proportional target, the employee's failure becomes difficult to characterize as individual underperformance.

**Metric:** Number of new client onboards across EMSX, RBLD, and RFQE product lines during the PIP period.

**Target:** 3 new client onboards in 90 days, with at least 1 reaching a bookable event (a forecast or first trade).

**Peer benchmark:** The company must know how many onboards each peer completed in the same 90-day period. If the employee meets this target but a peer with 3-5x the coverage does not onboard proportionally more, the target is validated as reasonable.

### 2.3 Objective 3: Client Activity and Coverage Breadth

**Metric:** Two sub-metrics.

**(a) C-day activity:** Number of C days (client-visit days) in the primary Connecticut territory, with a minimum number of in-person meetings and unique accounts visited per C day.

**Target (a):** A minimum of 6 C days in CT during the 90-day period, averaging at least 5 in-person meetings per C day across at least 3 unique accounts per C day.

**Target (b):** 85 unique firms engaged over 90 days.

**Justification:** The employee's H1 2025 data shows 177 unique firms engaged over approximately 6 months (~130 active working days, excluding weekends), which is approximately 29.5 unique firms per month or roughly 88 per quarter. **Metric:** Number of sustained strategic campaign adoptions (client workflows initiated through Bloomberg campaigns that result in continued use beyond the initial interaction).

**Peer benchmark:** What sustained adoption rates do peers achieve? The definition of "adoption" used in the PIP must match the definition used in evaluations and PIP criteria for all team members.

**Peer benchmark:** All peers' pipeline values at the same measurement point. Pipeline is a function of coverage breadth, so peer comparisons must account for coverage size.

Thirty-day PIPs are widely characterized by employment attorneys as "designed to fail" because they do not provide a genuine opportunity for improvement. **What happens if the employee fails:** The company proceeds to the next disciplinary step (extended PIP, reassignment, or separation), depending on the nature and degree of the shortfall. The specific next step should be determined in consultation with HR and employment counsel, not solely by the manager.

### 3.4 Attack: "Peers with similar metrics are not on PIPs -- selective application"

**Vulnerability:** High. This is a comparator-based attack that requires only one example to be effective. If any team member underperforms on any metric without facing a PIP or equivalent accountability, the employee's PIP looks targeted.

- If all peers are performing adequately on these metrics, the employee is genuinely the underperformer, and the PIP is justified.

## Section 5: What NOT to Do

These are common PIP mistakes that create legal liability, each analyzed for why it is dangerous in this specific case.

### 5.1 Do Not Set Targets the Employee Cannot Meet With Current Resources

**Why it's dangerous here:** The coverage disparity is quantified. A PIP with a $175,000 annualized revenue target for 1,138 firms when a peer needs the same target for 6,099 firms is a mathematical exhibit for the plaintiff. The employee predicted this. The employee's support team calls it an "engineered outcome." If the PIP proves the prediction, the case is substantially strengthened.

### 5.2 Do Not Use Metrics That Peers Also Fail to Meet

**Why it's dangerous here:** The employee claims he led the team in client activity (648 interactions, 178 visits, 177 unique firms in H1 2025). If the PIP includes activity metrics and the employee meets them while a peer does not -- and the peer faces no consequences -- the selective application argument is airtight.

**Prevention:** Before setting any metric in the PIP, pull current peer data. Every metric must be one where the employee is genuinely underperforming relative to peers, not one where the employee is actually at or above peer levels.

### 5.3 Do Not Implement Within 90 Days of Return From Protected Leave Without Documented Interim Feedback

**Why it's dangerous here:** The employee returned December 28, 2025. Implementing a PIP before late March 2026 creates a direct temporal proximity argument. Under NYCHRL's "motivating factor" standard, a PIP delivered within weeks of leave return, without documented interim feedback showing the employee was given a chance to improve, will be treated as retaliation.

**Prevention:** Follow the Section 4 timeline. The earliest defensible PIP implementation is June 2026, after the March expectations, April check-in, and May checkpoint have occurred.

### 5.4 Do Not Fail to Provide the Stated Support

**Why it's dangerous here:** If the PIP promises bi-weekly coaching sessions and the manager cancels three of them, the employee's attorney will argue the PIP was not genuine. If the PIP promises coverage review and no coverage is offered, same result.

**Prevention:** Every support element in Section 2.7 must actually be provided. Document each coaching session, each training opportunity, each introduction offered. If the employee declines support, document the declination. The support log is as important as the performance data.

### 5.5 Do Not Document Only Failures and Ignore Improvements

**Why it's dangerous here:** The employee's support team will argue the PIP documentation was designed to build a termination file, not to track genuine performance. If the checkpoint notes contain only criticisms and no acknowledgment of improvement or effort, a jury will agree.

**Prevention:** Checkpoint notes must be balanced. If the employee's activity metrics improve but revenue lags, say so. If the employee is making more C-day visits but not yet converting pipeline, acknowledge the effort while noting the gap. Balanced documentation is the hallmark of genuine performance management. One-sided documentation is the hallmark of pretextual PIPs.

### 5.6 Do Not Change the Success Criteria Mid-PIP

**Why it's dangerous here:** If the employee starts meeting targets and the manager raises the bar, the PIP becomes a moving goalpost. The employee's January 2026 prediction ("they'll say he needs to do A B C and then I'll say I didn't do a b c knowing nobody can") specifically anticipates impossible or shifting criteria.

**Prevention:** The PIP targets in Section 2 are fixed at implementation. They do not change. If the company determines the targets were too low, the appropriate response is to acknowledge PIP success and set higher expectations for the next period -- not to change the PIP mid-stream.

### 5.7 Do Not Use Subjective Criteria That Cannot Be Independently Verified

**Why it's dangerous here:** Criteria like "demonstrate stronger commitment" or "show improved attitude" or "engage more productively with clients" are subjective, unverifiable, and controllable by the evaluating manager. The employee's attorney will argue the manager can declare failure on subjective criteria regardless of the employee's actual effort.

**Prevention:** Every criterion in Section 2 has a numerical target tied to a Bloomberg system-generated measurement. "Revenue booked: $45,000" is verifiable. "Show improvement" is not.

### 5.8 Do Not Let the Manager Be the Sole Evaluator

**Why it's dangerous here:** The employee's core claim is that this specific manager retaliates against employees who take parental leave. If the same manager is the sole evaluator of PIP success or failure, the employee's attorney will argue the outcome was predetermined. The manager's alleged animus infects his judgment.

**Prevention:** HR must co-evaluate PIP performance. A second manager or senior leader should review the checkpoint data and the final assessment. System-generated metrics provide an objective layer that does not depend on the manager's subjective judgment.

### 5.9 Do Not Implement a PIP That Mirrors Sam Page's PIP

**Why it's dangerous here:** If the PIP structure, metrics, timeline, and outcome resemble what Sam Page experienced, the pattern evidence is confirmed. "Same manager, same process, same result for every employee who takes full leave."

**Prevention:** Before finalizing the PIP, review Sam Page's PIP documentation. Identify structural elements that differ. The model PIP in Section 2 includes several features designed to distinguish it: a 4-of-6 pass threshold (not all-or-nothing), employee self-assessment opportunities, documented support with a coaching log, HR co-administration, and explicit coverage equalization. If Page's PIP had none of these, the differentiation is clear.

### 5.10 Do Not Issue a PIP After an HR Retaliation Complaint Without Exhaustive Independent Review *(Added March 4)*

**Why it's dangerous here:** Nina R. Frank has specifically advised Sasha to file an HR complaint alleging retaliation before Bloomberg takes any further adverse action. Once that complaint is on file, a PIP becomes a separately actionable retaliatory act -- retaliation for complaining about retaliation. Under NYCHRL, filing an internal complaint is a protected activity. Adverse action after the complaint creates a second independent claim. The temporal proximity between complaint and PIP will be weeks, not months. There is no amount of PIP design sophistication that eliminates this inference.

**Prevention:** If the HR complaint has been filed, do not issue a PIP unless:

1. An independent review (not involving the manager or anyone in the manager's reporting chain) has concluded the PIP is warranted based on objective, system-verified performance data.
2. Bloomberg's employment counsel has reviewed the PIP, the HR complaint, the performance data, and the coverage disparity and has provided a written opinion that the PIP is defensible as a non-retaliatory action.
3. The PIP has been stress-tested against the specific attacks in Section 3.10.
4. The decision to PIP was documented as having been made or substantially initiated before the HR complaint was filed. If no such documentation exists, the PIP should not proceed.

If all four conditions cannot be met, the PIP should not be issued. The risk of a double-retaliation finding under NYCHRL is too high.

### 6.2 Factors Arguing For a PIP

**The employee may genuinely be underperforming.** High activity does not necessarily equal effective selling. If the employee is making many calls and visits but not converting pipeline, not driving sustained adoption, and not developing new revenue opportunities, a PIP is a legitimate management tool. The evaluation cites specific qualitative deficiencies (strategic adoptions were trials not sustained, C-day productivity was low, days were short) that activity volume does not address.

**Not addressing underperformance creates its own risk.** If the manager identifies performance issues and does nothing, the company is exposed to claims from other team members that standards are not applied consistently. A manager who fails to manage underperformance is not doing his job. The employee's leave status does not make him immune from accountability for genuine performance deficiencies.

**A PIP that the employee passes is the strongest possible defense.** If the PIP is genuinely achievable, and the employee succeeds, the company has demonstrated: (a) the process was fair, (b) the criteria were achievable, (c) the employee was given support and resources, and (d) the company's intent was improvement, not termination. A passed PIP largely defeats the retaliation narrative.

**Doing nothing may embolden the employee's legal position.** If the company backs off performance management entirely because the employee is building a legal case, the message is that threatening litigation grants immunity from accountability. This creates a precedent problem beyond this one employee.

### 6.3 Alternative Approaches

**Option 1: Informal coaching plan (NOT called a PIP).**

Replace the formal PIP with a documented coaching engagement. The manager (and ideally a second leader or HR partner) works with the employee on specific goals, checks in regularly, and documents progress. This achieves performance management without the legal trigger of a formal PIP. The employee's support team is specifically watching for a "PIP" -- they may not frame an informal coaching plan the same way.

*Advantages:* Avoids the word "PIP" and the formal termination path it implies. Harder for the employee to characterize as the next step in a managed-out playbook. Demonstrates good-faith management. If the employee improves, success. If the employee does not improve, the documentation trail still supports escalation to a formal PIP or other action later.

*Disadvantages:* Less structured. The employee's attorney will argue it is a "shadow PIP" designed to avoid the legal scrutiny of a formal one. If the company later escalates to a formal PIP, the informal period becomes evidence that the company knew a formal PIP was risky and tried to work around it.

**Option 2: Coverage restoration with clear expectations (no PIP, no coaching plan).**

Restore the employee's coverage to a level comparable to peers. Set clear, written expectations for 2026. Monitor and document performance through normal evaluation cycles. If performance remains below expectations at the next evaluation cycle (year-end 2026), address it then -- with the coverage disparity argument fully neutralized.

*Advantages:* Removes the most dangerous piece of evidence (coverage disparity) from the case. Gives the employee a genuine opportunity to perform with adequate resources. If the employee still underperforms with equalized coverage, the company's position is dramatically stronger at the next evaluation. The six-month lag also creates temporal distance from the leave return.

*Disadvantages:* Delays accountability for potentially genuine underperformance. If the employee's performance is legitimately poor, 10 months of documented non-action (from the Feb evaluation to the Dec year-end) weakens the company's argument that it takes performance seriously.

**Option 3: Monitored observation with documentation.**

Take no formal action. Monitor the employee's performance through normal channels. Document everything objectively. Pull system-generated metrics monthly. If the employee's Q1/Q2 2026 performance is genuinely subpar, the data will speak for itself and provide a much stronger PIP basis than the 2025 evaluation does. If the employee's performance is adequate or strong, no PIP is warranted and the company avoids an unnecessary legal fight.

*Advantages:* Lowest legal risk. The employee cannot claim a PIP is retaliatory if no PIP is implemented. The March meeting and normal check-ins still create a coaching dynamic. System-generated data from Q1/Q2 2026 will be the most powerful evidence in the company's arsenal -- far stronger than the half-year-compared-to-full-year data from the 2025 evaluation.

*Disadvantages:* No formal accountability mechanism. If the employee is building a case, the absence of a PIP does not stop the lawsuit. The employee's claims are based on the evaluations, coverage stripping, and manager statements -- none of which require a PIP to be actionable. The PIP, if it existed and the employee failed it, would actually provide the company with a performance-based justification for any eventual separation.
